JANUARY 2013 BOLIVIA WORK TRIP

Tina Manis, who has been with RVF for six years, kept a detailed diary during her visit to Montero. The excerpts below demonstrate the strong connection between the RVF board and the RVF children and their families.

Monday, Jan. 14th:
Today we met Yoselyn, 11, who has been under RVF’s care for five years. After contracting a severe fever when she was two years old, the result of an untreated infection, Yoselyn developed paralysis and had frequent seizures. Her body was rigid and she was unable to use her hands and fingers.  The RVF arranged intensive physical therapy for Yoselyn, and she is showing tremendous progress. She is able to grasp objects and loves to color. Yoselyn’s family was living in a shack, with mud walls and a dirt floor.  The RVF helped Yoselyn and her family move to a sturdy home with tiled floors, running water and electricity— a safer, more sanitary environment. Yoselyn’s father built a unique wheelchair and parallel bars to help his daughter develop leg muscles.  His obvious devotion to his daughter can only be described as “humbling”.

Tuesday, Jan. 15th:
It was a pleasure to finally meet Joaquin, 14, with his tremendous smile and warm, friendly handshake.  At age three, he started suffering from uncontrolled seizures.  When we first started working with him about eight years ago, Joaquin’s legs and arms were stiff and spastic and he couldn’t walk, speak, or even smile— all due to untreated seizures.  The RVF arranged intensive physical therapy, speech therapy and now monitors his anti-seizure medication regularly.  Today Joaquin was kicking a soccer ball around and begging Dr. Jon for a quick game! 

Weds., Jan. 16th:
This morning the hotel receptionist called to say that we [Tina and Karen] had a visitor.  It was Martha, who we met yesterday while checking in on her son Luis, a long-term RVF patient.  Martha got up before dawn, walked several miles to milk cows on a nearby farm, brought the milk back to her home and boiled it. (She had gone through all the trouble of boiling the milk first, as she knew our “delicate” stomachs wouldn’t be able to eat it otherwise.) Then she walked 2 miles to our hotel to bring us all the fixings for a Bolivian dessert delicacy:  milk, maize and raw sugar. All this before 6:15 a.m.!  Martha wanted us to know that she was grateful for the help the RVF had given her child.  We were so touched by her gratitude, and thrilled with the idea of dessert for breakfast!
